## DOC 1: Bridges Program Quarterly Report — Q1 2026

**Type:** Program Report
**Author:** Tara Whitford, Bridges Program Coordinator
**Date:** April 8, 2026
**Pages:** 4

### Program Overview

The Bridges Program serves youth aged 14 to 24 transitioning out of high school with co-occurring mental health and life skills needs. Q1 2026 enrolled 47 active participants across the King Edward Park and Mill Woods sites, with 12 youth on the waitlist as of March 31.

### Outcomes

Of the 47 active participants, 38 completed the quarterly check-in survey. Reported outcomes include 84% improved daily functioning since intake, 76% reduced anxiety symptoms, 71% would recommend the program to a friend, and average self-reported wellbeing scores increased from 4.2 to 6.8 out of 10.

Participant feedback was overwhelmingly positive. Several youth described the program as the only thing keeping them showing up. Another wrote that Bridges was a place where she actually felt heard for the first time since her mother passed.

### Staffing

The program is delivered by 3 full-time youth workers and 1 part-time clinical supervisor. We onboarded one new youth worker in February following the unexpected departure of a staff member in January. Staff retention remains a concern given Edmonton's competitive nonprofit labour market and our compensation falling below sector benchmarks.

### Concerns

The waitlist has grown 22% since Q4 2025. Without additional capacity, average wait time will exceed 8 weeks by Q3 2026. We are also seeing increased acuity at intake, with more participants reporting active suicidal ideation than in any previous quarter on record.

### Recommendations

We recommend exploring a third site location in north Edmonton, increasing clinical supervision hours from 0.5 FTE to 1.0 FTE, and revisiting compensation benchmarks for frontline staff.

## DOC 2: Anchor Program Quarterly Report — Q1 2026

**Type:** Program Report
**Author:** Devon Ng, Anchor Program Lead
**Date:** April 11, 2026
**Pages:** 3

### Program Overview

The Anchor Program provides drop-in mental health peer support for youth aged 16 to 22. Q1 2026 logged 312 unique visits across 14 weeks of programming at our Whyte Avenue location.

### Outcomes

Outcome data has been difficult to collect this quarter. Our intake survey response rate dropped to 34%, down sharply from 61% in Q4 2025. Of those who completed the survey, 58% reported the program was somewhat helpful, 22% reported it was very helpful, 14% reported it was not helpful, and 6% declined to answer.

Several youth raised concerns about wait times to speak with a peer support worker, with three separate complaints documented in the suggestion box. One youth wrote that the staff seem burnt out and rushed and another said it felt like nobody had time anymore.

### Staffing

Anchor is delivered by 2 full-time peer support workers and a rotation of 4 trained volunteers. We have struggled to fill an open peer support position since November 2025, now five months vacant. The team is operating at roughly 70% capacity with no end in sight.

### Concerns

Staff burnout is the most pressing issue I can identify. Both full-time workers have indicated they are considering leaving if conditions don't improve. I have flagged this to the Executive Director on three separate occasions since January and have not received a clear response.

The program is also facing a proposed 18% funding reduction in the 2026-27 budget, which I want to formally raise concern about. We discussed this in the March leadership meeting but I do not believe the implications for service delivery were fully understood at that time.

### Recommendations

Immediate hiring of a third peer support worker. Reconsideration of the proposed funding reduction. Wellness support and retention bonuses for current staff before we lose them.

## DOC 3: Compass Program Quarterly Report — Q1 2026

**Type:** Program Report
**Author:** Priya Sharma, Compass Program Manager
**Date:** April 14, 2026
**Pages:** 3

### Program Overview

Compass delivers school-based mental health workshops in partnership with Edmonton Public Schools and Edmonton Catholic Schools. Q1 2026 reached 1,847 students across 12 schools through 84 workshop sessions.

### Outcomes

Pre and post workshop surveys, administered to 1,612 students, showed 91% reported learning at least one new coping skill, 87% rated the workshop as good or excellent, and 79% indicated they would feel comfortable seeking help if needed. A small but important 4% reported feeling worse after the workshop, which prompted same-day follow-up referrals to school counsellors per our protocol.

Teacher feedback from 58 respondents was strongly positive, with 95% requesting return visits next school year. One teacher wrote that the workshops were the most engaged she had seen her students all year.

### Staffing

Compass is delivered by 5 part-time workshop facilitators with combined 2.4 FTE plus the program manager. Staffing has been stable throughout the quarter. Our facilitators are predominantly graduate students from the University of Alberta and MacEwan University, which provides good talent flow but creates expected turnover at academic year transitions.

### Concerns

None significant this quarter. The program is performing well within budget and on schedule. We continue to monitor the small percentage of students who report negative experiences and have refined our screening protocol accordingly.

### Recommendations

Expand to an additional 4 schools in the 2026-27 academic year. Consider piloting a parent-focused workshop series based on repeated requests from school administrators and counsellors.

## DOC 7: Bridges Case Summary — Anonymized Cohort Analysis Q1 2026

**Type:** Program Documentation
**Author:** Jordan Petit, Clinical Supervisor
**Date:** April 5, 2026
**Pages:** 4

### Methodology

This document summarizes anonymized clinical themes across 47 active Bridges participants in Q1 2026. All identifying information has been removed. This document does not constitute a clinical record and is for program planning purposes only.

### Presenting Concerns at Intake

Of the 47 participants, 32 presented with primary concerns related to anxiety, often co-occurring with school avoidance or social withdrawal. 28 presented with depressive symptoms ranging from mild to severe. 14 reported a history of self-harm. 11 disclosed active suicidal ideation at intake, which is the highest count we have recorded in any quarter. 9 reported substance use concerns. 6 had recently exited foster care. 4 disclosed experiences of family violence.

### Engagement Patterns

Average session attendance was 78%, comparable to historical norms. Participants who engaged in the optional peer mentorship component had 12% higher engagement than those who did not. Session-level dropout occurred most frequently in the first three weeks, consistent with prior cohorts.

### Outcomes at 12 Weeks

Of the 47 participants, 38 completed standardized outcome measures. Mean GAD-7 scores decreased from 14.2 at intake to 9.1 at 12 weeks. Mean PHQ-9 scores decreased from 13.8 at intake to 8.4 at 12 weeks. These changes are clinically meaningful.

### Clinical Observations

The cohort shows higher acuity at intake than previous quarters. Suicidal ideation disclosures have nearly doubled compared to Q1 2025 when 6 participants disclosed at intake. This may reflect either a true increase in community need or increased comfort disclosing in our intake process. Either interpretation warrants increased clinical capacity.

### Recommendations

Increase clinical supervision allocation. Consider piloting a brief crisis intervention protocol within Bridges for participants disclosing active ideation rather than referring out, given Edmonton's strained crisis services landscape.

## DOC 10: Bissell Cohort Outcomes Summary

**Type:** Program Documentation
**Author:** Tara Whitford and Devon Ng
**Date:** February 24, 2026
**Pages:** 3

### Background

In partnership with the Bissell Centre, Northbridge piloted a referral pathway for youth aging out of Bissell's family services into our Bridges and Anchor programs. The pilot ran from June 2025 through January 2026 and served 18 youth.

### Outcomes

Of the 18 referred youth, 16 completed an intake, 14 began programming, and 11 were retained at 12 weeks. Retention rate of 79% is strong for this population.

Of the 11 retained participants, outcome measures showed 9 with clinically meaningful improvement on standardized measures, 1 with stable scores, and 1 with worsening scores who was referred for higher-acuity care.

### Qualitative Themes

Participants from this cohort consistently named housing instability as their primary stressor, often above mental health concerns. Several participants noted that the combination of Bissell's housing supports with Northbridge's mental health programming felt more coherent than navigating separate systems. One youth wrote that for the first time it felt like the people helping her actually talked to each other.

### Recommendations

Formalize the referral partnership with a memorandum of understanding. Explore co-located service delivery at the Bissell Whyte Avenue location. This pilot has the strongest outcome data of any partnership initiative we have undertaken in the past three years.

## DOC 12: Compass Workshop Feedback Summary

**Type:** Program Documentation
**Author:** Priya Sharma, Compass Program Manager
**Date:** April 1, 2026
**Pages:** 2

### Methodology

Pre and post workshop surveys were administered to 1,612 of 1,847 students reached in Q1 2026. Teacher surveys were administered to all 58 partnering classroom teachers.

### Student Themes — Positive

Students consistently appreciated the use of relatable examples, the option to participate anonymously through written reflection, and the inclusion of practical coping techniques they could use the same day. Many noted that mental health was rarely discussed openly in their school environment and that the workshop normalized seeking help.

Selected anonymous student feedback included comments like the workshop made me feel like I'm not the only one, I learned that anxiety isn't weakness, and the breathing exercise actually helped during my math test.

### Student Themes — Critical

A small percentage of students reported feeling worse after the workshop. Common themes included feeling exposed by content that hit too close to home, frustration that the workshop ended without follow-up support, and one student wrote that talking about feelings just made me feel them more without anywhere to put them.

These responses prompted same-day referrals to school counselling and a refinement of our exit protocol to better signpost ongoing support options.

### Teacher Themes

Teachers were uniformly positive. 95% requested return visits. Common feedback included that students were more engaged than usual, that the facilitators were skilled with the age group, and that the workshops sparked productive classroom conversations in the days following.

One teacher noted that several students approached her privately in the days after the workshop to disclose concerns they had not previously raised.
